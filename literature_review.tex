\section{Literature Review}

Hallucinations in Large Language Models (LLMs) have become a major concern as these models are increasingly used in knowledge-intensive applications. Researchers have proposed several approaches to detect and measure hallucinations, including reference-based evaluation, self-consistency methods, and probabilistic modeling techniques.

\subsection{Belief Tree Propagation}

Belief Tree Propagation (BTPROP), proposed by Hou et al.~\cite{hou2025btprop}, (2025), is a probabilistic framework designed to detect hallucinations in LLM-generated text. The method constructs a belief tree by decomposing a statement into multiple related claims. Each claim is assigned a probability representing the model’s confidence in its correctness. These probabilities are then combined through probabilistic inference using a hidden Markov tree structure to estimate the factual reliability of the original statement. By reasoning over the relationships between claims, BTPROP can identify inconsistencies and improve hallucination detection performance compared to baseline approaches.

\textbf{Datasets.}Datasets. To evaluate BTPROP and similar approaches, we follow previous
work on hallucination detection \cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt, chen2023felm}
and use the following datasets: Wikibio-GPT3 \cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt},
FELM-Science \cite{chen2023felm}, and FactCheckGPT \cite{wang2023factcheckgpt}.
Wikibio-GPT3 mainly consists of biography articles generated by LLMs,
whereas FELM-Science and FactCheckGPT cover a broader range of topics,
including physics, chemistry, and computer science.

\subsection{HaloScope}

HaloScope is a framework for detecting hallucinations in large language model
(LLM) generations using unlabeled data in the wild. Unlike traditional approaches
relying on costly human-labeled datasets, it leverages latent representations of
LLM outputs to distinguish truthful from hallucinated content. HaloScope was
evaluated with LLaMA-2-chat (7B \& 13B) and OPT (6.7B \& 13B) models under
zero-shot inference, and compared against uncertainty-based, consistency-based,
prompting-based, and knowledge discovery baselines. Experimental results
demonstrate that HaloScope substantially improves hallucination detection
performance~\cite{du2024haloscope}.

\textbf{Datasets.} We evaluate HaloScope on four generative QA tasks: open-book conversational QA datasets COQA~\cite{reddy2019coqa} and TRUTHFULQA~\cite{lin2022truthfulqa}, closed-book QA dataset TRIVIAQA~\cite{joshi2017triviaqa}, and reading comprehension dataset TYDIQAGP~\cite{clark2020tydiqa}. Dataset sizes are 817 QA pairs (TRUTHFULQA), 3,696 (TYDIQAGP), 7,983 (COQA development split), and 9,960 (TRIVIAQA rc.nocontext validation split). We reserve 25\% for testing, 100 for validation, and use the remaining to simulate unlabeled generations, with greedy sampling by default.

\subsection{Lowest Span Confidence (LSC)}

Lowest Span Confidence (LSC) \cite{qiao2024lsc} is a zero-shot metric designed for hallucination detection in LLMs under minimal resource assumptions. Unlike methods that require multiple samples or white-box access, LSC needs only a single forward pass and the output token probabilities. It evaluates the joint likelihood of semantically coherent spans using a sliding window over variable-length n-grams. The span with the lowest aggregated confidence highlights localized uncertainty strongly correlated with factual inconsistencies. This approach mitigates the dilution effect of perplexity and the noise sensitivity of minimum token probability, offering a robust and efficient metric for black-box, API-based deployments of large language models.

\textbf{Datasets.} LSC is evaluated on four widely adopted Question Answering benchmarks: Natural Questions (NQ) \cite{kwiatkowski2019natural} validation split with 3,610 QA pairs, TriviaQA \cite{joshi2017triviaqa} rc.nocontext validation set containing 9,960 examples, SQuAD 2.0 \cite{rajpurkar2018squad2} development split filtered for answerable queries totaling 5,928 samples, and CoQA \cite{reddy2019coqa} development split with 7,983 conversational QA pairs. These datasets cover a diverse range of sequence lengths and question types, providing a robust testbed for hallucination detection.

\subsection{Research Gaps}

Despite significant progress in hallucination detection for Large Language Models (LLMs), several limitations remain in existing approaches. First, probabilistic frameworks such as Belief Tree Propagation rely on structured claim decomposition and probabilistic inference over belief trees. While this approach improves reasoning over factual claims, it often requires complex modeling assumptions and additional computational overhead, which may limit its scalability for large-scale applications.

Second, recent frameworks such as HaloScope demonstrate strong performance by leveraging latent representations and unlabeled data to detect hallucinations. However, these approaches often depend on sophisticated representation learning and model-specific characteristics, which may reduce their generalizability across different model architectures and deployment settings.

Third, many hallucination detection techniques rely on multiple sampling strategies, self-consistency checks, or additional prompting mechanisms to evaluate the reliability of generated responses. Although these techniques can improve detection accuracy, they significantly increase inference time and computational cost, making them less suitable for real-time or resource-constrained environments.

Furthermore, existing evaluation strategies often depend on lexical or semantic similarity metrics such as ROUGE or embedding similarity. These metrics primarily capture surface-level textual similarity and may fail to accurately identify deeper factual inconsistencies or reasoning errors present in generated outputs.

Finally, while recent methods such as Lowest Span Confidence (LSC) aim to address efficiency and black-box deployment constraints, there remains a need for more comprehensive frameworks that can balance detection accuracy, computational efficiency, and applicability across diverse tasks and datasets. Addressing these challenges is essential to improve the reliability and trustworthiness of large language models in real-world applications.
